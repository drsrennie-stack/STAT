Are you actually in critical mode?

Five questions before you enroll. STAT Success Cohort is built for one specific kind of student: a student in academic critical care. The questions below will tell you whether that is you, or whether a different kind of support will serve you better right now.

Two modes, briefly

A full explanation of the critical care framework lives in the welcome lesson. The short version is below, just enough to make the self-check make sense.

Preventative mode
Stable patient.

Grades are at or above passing. Methods are working. Time horizon is long. Small adjustments, slow compounding. Most academic support is built for this mode.

Critical mode
Unstable patient.

Grades are below passing or actively sliding. Usual methods have stopped working. Time horizon is weeks, not semesters. Direct intervention, immediate action, coordinated team. STAT is built for this.

The self-check

Five questions. Each one targets a different validated dimension of academic crisis: where you are, how much time you have, whether your usual tools still work, whether you can act on what you already know, and what is at stake if recovery does not happen. Three or more "yes" answers indicates critical mode.

Your responses stay in your browser. Nothing is submitted, stored, or sent.

Question 01
Are you currently below passing in at least one science or health science course, with not enough remaining points to recover passively?
Screening for: current academic standing
Question 02
Is there a critical assessment in the next 4 to 8 weeks that you must pass to recover the course?
Screening for: time pressure
Question 03
Have your usual study methods stopped working this semester despite real effort?
Screening for: method exhaustion (self-regulated learning failure)
Question 04
Do you find yourself unable to start studying, even when you have time and you know what you should be doing?
Screening for: initiation deficit (academic procrastination, executive dysfunction)
Question 05
Are the consequences of failing this course significant beyond the grade itself? (program admission, GPA cutoff, financial aid, graduation timeline, professional licensure)
Screening for: stakes and goal commitment

What this self-check is screening for

Each question targets a clinically validated construct that predicts who actually benefits from a structured, intensive recovery program versus a lighter intervention. STAT is intentionally calibrated for students who score positively on three or more.

  • Q1: StandingWhere you are right now, mathematically. Below passing with insufficient remaining points is a different problem than feeling behind.
  • Q2: Time pressureRecovery windows determine intervention intensity. A 4 to 8 week window calls for structure that a slower program cannot deliver.
  • Q3: Method failureIf your usual study methods are still working, you do not need structural intervention. You need refinement. STAT is built for students whose methods have stopped working.
  • Q4: Initiation deficitThe gap between knowing what to do and doing it is one of the most validated predictors of academic failure. External structure closes that gap. Information alone does not.
  • Q5: StakesGoal commitment scales with consequence weight. Students with significant stakes engage with intensive programs differently than students for whom the grade is incidental.

This is not a diagnostic instrument. It is a self-check, intended to help you decide whether STAT is the right fit before you commit time and money. If your situation does not show three or more positive responses, STAT will be the wrong tool, and a different kind of support will serve you better.

Two doors

Pick the one that fits where you actually are.

If your self-check pointed to critical mode, the welcome lesson is the next step. If it pointed to preventative mode, your time and energy are better spent on a different program. Honest self-routing now saves wasted effort later.
